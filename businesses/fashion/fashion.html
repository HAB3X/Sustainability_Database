    About the fashion industry

    The fashion industry covers the design, production, marketing, and sale of clothing, footwear, and accessories. It is one of the world's largest industries by employment and economic output, but also among the most resource intensive. The sector spans fast fashion, luxury design, local manufacturing, and emerging sustainable labels.

    Fashion intersects directly with global sustainability challenges. Its supply chains depend on raw materials such as cotton, wool, and synthetics, often linked to high water use, chemical pollution, and labour issues. Sustainable fashion therefore seeks to balance aesthetics and affordability with responsibility toward people, animals, and the planet.

    Key sustainability factors

    When assessing sustainability within the fashion sector, this database evaluates companies on factors such as:

    • Transparency in sourcing and manufacturing processes.
    • Use of sustainable and recycled materials (for example organic cotton, hemp, Tencel, recycled polyester).
    • Fair labour practices, worker safety, and equitable wages.
    • Waste reduction through circular models such as repair, resale, and recycling.
    • Minimising environmental impact across dyeing, water use, and carbon footprint.
    • Longevity and quality of products to avoid disposable consumption patterns.
    • Animal welfare and avoidance of fur, exotic skins, or unethical materials.

    Common certifications and sustainable practices

    Recognised certifications and sustainability initiatives include:

    • Global Organic Textile Standard (GOTS): certifies organic fibres and ethical processing.
    • Fair Trade Certified: ensures fair wages and working conditions.
    • B Corp Certification: measures social and environmental performance across businesses.
    • OEKO TEX Standard 100: verifies textiles free from harmful substances.
    • Carbon neutral / Climate Active: recognises verified emissions measurement and offsetting.
    • Australian Made Campaign: supports on shore manufacturing and shorter supply chains.

    Additional best practices include local production, closed loop fabric systems, second hand and vintage marketplaces, and education campaigns promoting responsible consumption.
